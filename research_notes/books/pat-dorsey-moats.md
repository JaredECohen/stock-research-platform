# Pat Dorsey: Moat Durability Heuristics

The four-source taxonomy is well-known. What's underappreciated is the **durability**
test — most "moats" in screens are fragile, regression-to-the-mean stories that
look like moats only because the screening window happened to capture an
above-average year.

## The five tests

1. **Switching costs** — does the customer pay a real cost to leave (data, retraining, integration)?
2. **Network effects** — does each new participant raise the value for incumbents?
3. **Intangible assets** — patents, regulatory moats, brand premium that buyers actually pay for.
4. **Cost advantages** — process, scale, or location that competitors can't replicate.
5. **Efficient scale** — markets where the incumbent's economics deter entry by virtue of small TAM.

## How to apply

When the cohort says a name's gross margin is in the top quartile, ask which
of the five sources is producing the premium. If you can't name one, the
quartile placement is suspect — it's likely a timing artifact rather than a
moat.

When the comps say a stock is at a premium multiple, the question isn't "is the
premium deserved?" — it's "is the moat that produces the premium *durable*?"
Premium without durability is volatility risk wearing a costume.
